(Empire) State of Mind

What the fuck, day? Sometimes things happen to me and I think, "How do I write this into a college essay?"

Now everybody report to the dance floor. We beat on, boats against the current, born back ceaselessly into the past.

I'm not a writer and I think the two lines above demonstrate that pretty well, but let me try to tell you a little bit of a story. Let me tell you to ask yourself the right questions. Don't ask yourself how to write things into a college essay. Don't ask yourself how to get into MIT and Harvard. Just... do things. Do what you want to do. Forget rules — start an article with swearing if it’s what you want to do.

I'll be hood forever

When I was very, very young, I lived in New York City. After that, I lived in Berkeley. In both cases, my parents — intelligent, college-educated, employed — struggled to get by. My father worked at NYU and UC Berkeley (schools I've spent the last four years trying to get into), and despite a frugal lifestyle, my parents lived with money in the negatives. Their single-parent friends had thousands of dollars to spare after tax deductions, food programs, and other government subsidies: but my family worked hard at the forefront of academia and couldn't find a single dollar to spare.

My parents built themselves out of nothing. I know what a dollar means. I know the value of money and of education because my parents are a model story of success: they were the best in their schools in Russia, risked everything to move to the United States, and put everything into their child's education. I went to a private school while my parents lived paycheck to paycheck. To them, it was simple: linear algebra at Stanford? Take it. Doesn't matter how much it costs. New pants? Do I really need them?

They put everything on the line so I might have a better future. And I know I write this as a white kid in Palo Alto, California, but still: I'll be hood forever. I know where I came from. I know the sacrifices other people made to bring me here.

City of Sin and you're a virgin.

So what the hell was today? I went to my dual-enrollment research class. I went to my honors foreign policy class. I skipped college-level English Literature so I could go to the DMV in the middle of the day to take a driver's permit test (and got one question wrong — embarrassing).

When I walked out of the testing area holding my permit (yes, proudly, even if that's cliché), I saw my dad at the table where my mom was waiting. It's my mom's week, so he shouldn't have been there. Plus, they've been separated for a year (yeah, try doing junior year of high school while sleeping on a mattress on the floor because your parents just separated), but whatever. I thought he was there to celebrate.

Silly me.

They were just signing formal asset separation papers; my father had sold his company for millions and entered the C-Suite of a very high-profile software corporation (unnamed to avoid illegal market trading). That's what it means to give everything to a cause, to never give up, and to put something first.

I'm proud of him; that's who I want to be.

But every success has a dark story behind it. Zuckerberg revolutionized the internet, but it meant he lost Eduardo Saverin as a friend. Even Google — "Don't be evil" — has dark stories behind it: hundreds of lawsuits, some even reaching the Supreme Court (yeah, the Java programming language has seen a lot), are what built the company. Today, my father sold his company, but it meant my family is split across two houses. At least my immediate family is. My other relatives lives in Israel and Russia — yes, that's two active warzones. Does good ever exist without an equal part of evil behind it? Yin and Yan.

As Viktor says in Arcane: "What inspires us to our greatest good is also the cause of our greatest evil. Two sides of the same coin, inextricably bound."

That's why every place in the world is just a City of Sin: we can't talk about it. We celebrate success but ignore the costs. We tout success as well-defined. Looks, luck, money, and never left a-wishing, right?

So what does it really mean to succeed? I've spent four years going to sleep before 10pm. College-level and dual-enrollment homework always done days ahead of its due date. Homework in high school? Never seen that. I taught myself six college courses (economics, calculus, physics...). I wrote code in seven programming languages and worked on operating systems and open-source intelligence tooling with professionals. I literally made a tool that locates possible positions of shadows, like, three times faster. I worked at Lockheed Martin's Advanced Technology Center and implemented a CSV reader 980 times faster than built-in Python libraries.

The vast majority of my classmates have broken the law. Skipped class. Shoplifted. Gotten drunk. Vaped. Every high school is the same: police don't care. It's a city of sin, and I'm a virgin. I don't even drink caffeine.

I know what that sounds like. I'm not trying to show off and do some virtue-signalling-on-LinkedIn or anything; I'm showing you what it means to be what people expect you to be. It means wondering, when your parents sign separation papers while you're at the DMV, "What if I did break the law?"

What if I did just... stop trying? Stopped pushing myself to every limit I know of, and just... lived life?

Eight billion stories, right there in the making.

I might never know the answer to what it's like to break the law — Eminem has a whole song about this, "Just Lose It" — and let go of all my expectations, but I'll survive (like I always have).

Back to the DMV and the fact that everyone, even the perfect fucking nerd, has a story that nobody else can tell. When I was in line, there was an old lady in front of me and a high school student from San Fransisco behind me. The lady had worked at Lockheed Martin for 49 and a quarter years.

Whoa. Small world. I quit my job at Lockheed less than a week ago. The people waiting in line to my left were told not to eat — they were eating Taco Bell — by a security guard. Really small world. I had tried Taco Bell for the first time just four days before that appointment.

The person in front of me at check-in was reporting an accident to the DMV, and... it was his birthday. Happy belated birthday, man.

The truth is, everyone has a story like mine. I'm not special. I can tell you about a "What the fuck" day, and someone can tell you about a day four times worse.

Street lights, big dreams, all looking pretty...

Everybody has a story to tell. I know that: I'm an editor for the Journal for Youth Voice. Everyone has a story, but more importantly, everybody has a future. What are you going to do next? When you see your parents formalizing their divorce while you celebrate something you worked hard to achieve, what do you do next? Where do you steer your one and only life?

In my case, I went to robotics. I want to win the World Championship. I'm going to fight tooth and nail (braces not included, I just got them removed!) to make it happen because my parents showed me what it's like to work hard and have it pay off.

And yeah: my dinner was leftovers from my teammate's family dinner. And yeah: I was the last one to leave because my mom works until 9pm. And yeah: I have school tomorrow.

And since I made it here, I can make it anywhere.

I might never try alcohol, or get invited to a rager, or steal snacks from CVS. I might not win the World Championship. I might not get into NYU or UC Berkeley. The company I started after quitting my job at Lockheed (seriously: what kind of high school student quits a job at Lockheed Martin?) may never bring in even a single cent of revenue.

But if today taught me anything, it's that everybody is singular. Every single story is, literally, a one-in-eight-billion story. What will mine be in the story they will write someday?
