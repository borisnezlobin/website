A Justification

How many years would it take you to do everything you have ever wanted to do in life?

How many days would you need to spend on this Earth before you could say that there is nothing left for you to try? That you have learned how to code; that you have tried paragliding and llama-riding in the Alps; that you have visited both Poles and been to space?

The basic premise I am trying to get across is simple: you only get one life. There will never be another, and there will never be enough time. If you were hospitalized with Stage Four cancer tomorrow and given a week to live, you would have regrets. You would regret not apologizing to people you’ve hurt, not “locking in” to make billions when you had the chance, and even that you never visited Singapore.

Is this a reason to lose hope and never try anything? Is it reason to say, “I can’t learn guitar; I won’t have time to learn how to surf”?

I’m in high school. I don’t know.

But when—if I make it till then—I’m seventy years old and looking back at my life, I want to think, know, that there was no better way I could have spent my time.

A Critique

I am, at the time of writing, in high school. I am a rat, racing other rats to get out of a maze. We do not know which direction to take but we have been told that the ones ahead of us took a certain path—they played a sport, did community service, had good grades—and got out of the maze. So we follow that path.

But I have never gone to (well, I’ve never been invited) to a rager. I have never gotten drunk. I have never played basketball in the park with my friends until two in the morning. At the same time, I haven’t devoted myself wholeheartedly to the pursuit of a single passion: I am not a thirteen-year-old chess grandmaster, nor am I an Olympic athlete. I haven’t broken ten thousand hours in sports, music, or any other subject (though I’m getting close with programming, lol).

The truth is, most high school students are like me (in some respect or another). Most are trying to be the best at six or seven separate extracurriculars—I have seen quite enough President-of-Math-Club-is-Captain-of-the-volleyball-team-esque situations—in the hopes of getting into a good university so that they can get a good job and make a lot of money, then retire comfortably.

You and I only live once. If you don’t love running a Math Club, why do it? Why spend hundreds of hours volunteering if you don’t love the cause? Four (or forty) years from now, will you think that you had a better use for your time?

“If only I had been learning and practicing neuroscience in high school instead of volunteering at Speech and Debate outreach events, I could have changed the world.”

And that is true. You could’ve.

A List

It’s necessary to define what a “successful” life is to you. Is it really studying and working hard for decades just so you can retire when you’re 65 and play golf with arthritis? For most people, it might be.

But for me, it is not. I want to change the world. I can do it if I just shut the fuck up and focus.

I will believe that I have lived a successful life if, by the end of it, I have:

  • Become one of the five richest people on the planet,
    • (and die having given the vast majority of it away/invested it in projects to improve as much as possible for as many people as possible)
  • Implemented a new social culture in a significant community/area of land (hopefully Mars); one that addresses deep issues of inequality and how we educate our children with the wrong values (more on that elsewhere, sometime), and
  • have no enemies. I should be able to call every person I have ever met and expect them to pick up the phone (perhaps even attend my funeral).

And I will wake up tomorrow with eight and a half hours of sleep with one goal: achieve these three goals.

A Promise

To the people who deserve it: I’m sorry. To the people who doubt me: thank you. To the people who support me: I’ll be hood forever.

We think that “tomorrow” is a fresh start. It isn’t. What we do today decides what we do tomorrow—but that means we can change the future with how we act today. I have listed my goals, and I will pursue them until I achieve them or physically cannot continue. I will change my future with how I act today.

Forty years from now, I will not think that I could have worked harder. I will not think that I could have made more sacrifices.

I promise that.
Boris Nezlobin.
